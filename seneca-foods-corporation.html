Section 8 · Recommended Sales Plays
Play 1 · Cumberland Lever → MN Site Expansion
Expansion

RMH has a live PKG relationship at the Cumberland, WI plant. The fastest path to sites 2–17 is through that relationship — not a cold call into Minnesota. This play uses the existing account as a lever to get a warm introduction to Seneca's MN operations.

  • Confirm the Cumberland relationship is healthy before expanding. Call or visit the Cumberland contact and validate: Is the PKG installation performing well? Has their team been happy with RMH's service response? Any unresolved issues? A strained relationship at site 1 will torpedo the expansion conversation before it starts.
  • Ask directly: "Who runs your Minnesota plants?" The magic question is: "Seneca has a number of plants in Minnesota that are very similar to Cumberland — do you know who handles equipment and maintenance decisions out there? I'd love an introduction." If they're willing, one email from the Cumberland contact to a Blue Earth or Lake Elmo plant manager is worth 10 cold calls.
  • Introduce MHE and Robotics as the cross-sell from PKG. At Cumberland you did packaging. At Blue Earth and Lake Elmo, lead with MHE and Robotics — both represent the largest unrealized potential ($132K combined) and food manufacturing operations in those plants will have immediate context for the ROI argument.
  • Quantify using Cumberland. "At Cumberland we've been providing [PKG service/product] for 7 months. If we could bring that same relationship to your Blue Earth and Lake Elmo plants, the model suggests there's roughly $35K–$97K in MHE and automation potential per year — across just those two sites."
  • Ask for a plant visit, not a Zoom call. Food manufacturing decision-making happens on the plant floor, not in conference rooms. Ask for a 45-minute walk-through at one MN site. "Would you be open to having us visit Blue Earth so we can see your lines and come back with a specific recommendation?" This is the next step that counts.
Play 2 · Robotics ROI for Seasonal Peak Operations
New Logo (Product Line)

Seneca operates high-volume harvest-season production cycles — peak periods where labor constraints and throughput pressure are most acute. This play positions RMH Robotics specifically against that operational challenge, and can be run independently of the Cumberland relationship with any MN or WI plant manager.

  • Lead with the harvest-season labor challenge, not the robot. Open with: "We work with a number of food manufacturers in Minnesota and Wisconsin — canning and frozen in particular. The consistent theme we hear in August through October is that throughput on the packing line is the constraint, not the harvest. We've been helping companies solve that with automation that pays for itself before the end of the season."
  • Ask: "What does your peak season look like, and where does the line slow down?" Get them to articulate their own throughput problem. Ideal answer: a palletizing or end-of-line bottleneck during corn, pea, or bean pack season. That is the exact use case for RMH Robotics.
  • Position RMH Robotics against the labor cost, not the capital cost. In food manufacturing, the CFO question is: "What does a season of overtime and temp labor cost versus a robotic palletizer?" Frame the ROI as a labor displacement calculation, not a technology investment. For a $1.58B company, the capital approval threshold is not the obstacle — labor cost certainty is the argument.
  • Use the $97K modeled potential as a conversation anchor. "Based on what comparable food manufacturers of your size have done with us, there's typically $75K–$100K per year in robotics and automation opportunities across a plant of this type. We'd want to validate that on-site — does that scale of investment have a path to approval at this plant?"
  • Close on a site assessment, not a proposal. "The most useful thing we can do at this stage is spend two hours on your line and come back to you with a prioritized recommendation — no cost, no commitment. Would next Tuesday or Thursday work for a walk-through?"

Section 10 · Open Questions for First Call
  1. Who is the primary contact at the Cumberland, WI plant who authorized the PKG purchase — and are they still in that role? Is that relationship in good standing before we try to expand it?
  2. Which equipment vendors or distributors does Seneca currently use for Cranes, conveyors, and MHE at the MN plants — and is there an incumbent relationship that's locked in, or are these sites buying opportunistically?
  3. How does capital equipment approval work at Seneca — does a plant manager have authority to approve a $50–100K MHE or Robotics purchase, or does it go to a regional or corporate ops leader in Fairport?
  4. What does Seneca's peak production season look like at the MN sites — and where are the current throughput or labor constraints on the packing and palletizing end of the line?
  5. Are the MN and WI plants on a maintenance budget cycle that aligns to a calendar year or fiscal year — and is there remaining budget available in 2025, or is this a 2026 conversation?
  6. Has Seneca evaluated robotics or automation at any of the MN sites in the last 2–3 years? If so, what happened — was it evaluated and deferred, or is it a greenfield conversation at this plant?
  7. Is there a Seneca corporate procurement or preferred vendor program that RMH would need to qualify for before selling across multiple sites — or can each plant manager engage RMH independently?
